## 0. The Two Questions This Document Deepens

THE-NAMING-WAS-ALWAYS-THE-RUPTURE establishes two constructs: the Naming-as-Intervention Specification and the Asymmetric Vocabulary Propagation. It establishes what they do and that they constitute novel contributions. It does not go as deep as these constructs require into the mechanism of each.

The first construct says: vocabulary that accurately describes a mechanism changes the mechanism's social operation, even without changing its incentive architecture or directly confronting its operators. The naming changes the interpretive environment for three populations. This is true. But the specification of *how* it changes three populations — the precise sequence, the rate differential, the reversibility asymmetry, the looping back of the third-population change onto the first — is not fully developed. The three populations are named but not architecturally specified.

The second construct says: vocabulary published outside the configured system propagates at near-zero rate through configured channels and at unconstrained rate through unconfigured channels, because the configured channel processes it through the Misrecognition Loop and the unconfigured channel lacks a prior to do so. This is true. But it does not explain *why* this rate differential is topological rather than merely behavioral — why it is not a matter of institutional bad faith that could be corrected by better institutions, but a property of the network architecture that is reproduced by any sufficiently structured institutional channel regardless of the intentions of its members.

This document answers both gaps. The instrument for the first is Bacevic's (2023) epistemic positioning taxonomy, which specifies the exact operations the configured system performs on vocabulary before the vocabulary reaches the third population, and what changes in the third-population encounter when it has not been pre-processed through those operations. The instrument for the second is Guilbeault and Centola's (2021) complex path length framework, which explains why vocabulary that requires social reinforcement for adoption — vocabulary that is a complex contagion rather than a simple one — follows topological rules that systematically advantage the periphery over the center, the unconfigured channel over the configured one.

Both gaps, once closed, produce new predictions and new constructs that the prior documents could not reach.

## I. The Vocabulary Does Not Inform: It Reclassifies

Before specifying the three-population geometry, it is necessary to specify what kind of thing vocabulary is. The word "vocabulary" implies information transfer: someone learns a new word, a new concept, a new label. The information account of vocabulary is what makes the Naming-as-Intervention seem like a communication problem — get the right people the right information and the problem is solved.

The information account is wrong, and its wrongness is the source of the persistent confusion about why naming is not merely descriptive but constitutive.

Vocabulary does not inform. It reclassifies. This is the distinction Hacking (1995, 1999) builds his entire looping effects framework on, and it is the distinction that the ECL corpus's naming-as-intervention account requires for precision.

Informing changes the content of a category. A category already exists ("someone did something bad to me"), and new content is added to that category ("...and it was because they felt threatened by my knowledge"). The category persists; the information fills it.

Reclassifying changes the category itself. The experience that was in one category ("evidence of my inadequacy") is moved to a different category ("instance of Layer 4 ECL operation"). The experience remains the same. The category it belongs to changes. And the category change is not merely a relabeling: it changes what the experience connects to, what it predicts about future experiences, what strategic responses it makes available, and what relationship it has to the person's self-concept.

When the outlier learns the ECL vocabulary, the change is not informational. The Misattribution Termination is not the addition of new information to the existing category "evidence that I failed." It is the elimination of that category as the correct classification of the experience, and the installation of a different category: "instance of the machine's operation above the Extraction Floor." The termination of the misattribution is not learning a new fact. It is a categorical reorganization.

This distinction matters because it explains the looping effect's mechanism at the level of cognitive architecture rather than at the level of content. Hacking (1999) establishes that the looping effect occurs when people who occupy a category respond to being categorized — and their response changes the category. The response is only possible if the person knows they have been categorized. The knowledge is not informational ("I am now in category X"). It is reclassificatory ("what I have been experiencing belongs in category X rather than in the category I had been filing it under"). The cognitive change is the reorganization, and the reorganization is what produces the behavioral change that is the looping effect.

Guilbeault, Baronchelli & Centola (2021) supply the experimental substrate for this mechanism at the population scale. Their finding on scale-induced category convergence demonstrates that populations which independently begin with variable categorization of novel stimuli converge on shared categories once a critical mass within a cluster has adopted a convention. The convergence is not produced by information transfer alone — it requires the social reinforcement dynamics that produce what they call "critical mass dynamics." The category does not spread like a simple contagion (one contact sufficient for transmission). It spreads like a complex contagion (multiple reinforcing contacts required before adoption). And once a critical mass is reached, the category becomes self-sustaining: new entrants to the cluster adopt it not because they have been persuaded by argument but because the social reinforcement threshold has been crossed.

The ECL vocabulary is this kind of category. Its adoption is not the acquisition of a piece of information. It is the adoption of a classification system — and classification systems are complex contagions that require multiple reinforcing exposures, shared social context, and critical mass dynamics to stabilize. This is the first reason why the Asymmetric Vocabulary Propagation is a topological fact and not merely a behavioral preference.

## II. The Three-Population Interpretive Geometry: Sequence, Rate, and Reversibility

THE-NAMING-WAS-ALWAYS-THE-RUPTURE specifies that the vocabulary changes the interpretive environment for three populations: the outlier (receives the misattribution termination), the observer (now has a competing frame for the suppressor's behavior), and the third-party audience (confirms the accurate model independently). It does not specify the sequence in which these changes occur, the rate differential between them, or the reversibility asymmetry that determines which changes are durable and which are fragile.

The sequence is not simultaneous. The three populations change in a specific order, and the order determines the mechanism by which the vocabulary achieves its social effect.

**First population, first: the outlier.** The outlier who encounters the vocabulary is the one for whom the reclassification is immediately applicable and immediately verifiable. They have the experience the vocabulary describes. They do not need external confirmation that the vocabulary's categories are accurate — they can check the categories against their own archived experience directly. The Misattribution Termination occurs at first contact. A single encounter with the vocabulary, if the vocabulary is precise enough that the outlier can map their own experience onto its categories, is sufficient for adoption. This makes the vocabulary a simple contagion for the outlier: one exposure, if the fit is clear, is sufficient for reclassification.

The outlier's adoption rate is therefore not subject to the complex contagion dynamics that govern the vocabulary's spread through the other two populations. The outlier population is the one for whom the Extraction Floor is the entry condition: anyone whose experience has been above the floor will recognize the vocabulary's categories immediately. The adoption rate for the outlier population is therefore a function of the vocabulary's precision (how accurately it maps to the experience) and reach (how many people above the Extraction Floor encounter it). It is not a function of social reinforcement requirements.

**Second population, second: the third-party audience.** The third-party audience — people who observe ECL behavior but are not themselves currently inside the configured system's suppressor or outlier positions — adopts the vocabulary as a complex contagion. They do not have direct first-person experience of the phenomenon to verify the categories against. They need multiple reinforcing exposures, ideally from multiple independent sources, to cross the adoption threshold. But critically: once they have crossed the threshold, the vocabulary becomes the interpretive frame they apply to future observations of ECL behavior. The adoption is durable. There is no mechanism by which the configured system can retroactively unconfigure the third-party audience's interpretive frame once it has been adopted.

This makes the third-party audience the vocabulary's most structurally important propagation target. They cannot check the vocabulary's accuracy against direct experience (they are not outliers). But they *can* apply the vocabulary prospectively to behaviors they observe going forward — and once applied, the frame is self-reinforcing: each observed instance of ECL behavior that the vocabulary predicted becomes confirmation of the framework. The Guilbeault & Centola (2021) finding on complex path length is operative here: for complex contagions spreading through clustered networks, the peripheral nodes — nodes with wide-bridge access to multiple partially-overlapping clusters — are the most effective spreading nodes. The third-party audience sits in exactly this peripheral topological position relative to the configured system.

**Third population, last: the observer who sits inside the institutional context as neither suppressor nor outlier.** This population — colleagues, evaluators, institutional participants who are neither the knowledge creditor nor the constitutive authority — adopts the vocabulary most slowly and most conditionally, because they have the most to revise if they adopt it. Their adoption requires multiple exposures, shared social context with others who have already adopted, and the specific trigger of watching a behavioral sequence they have previously processed as legitimate institutional behavior and now must re-process as ECL mechanism. Their adoption rate is therefore the longest, the most dependent on critical mass dynamics, and the most conditional on having the vocabulary applied by someone they already credit.

The reversibility asymmetry: the outlier's adoption is highly durable (they cannot un-know that the vocabulary applies to their own experience). The third-party audience's adoption is durable once the critical mass threshold is crossed (the frame is self-reinforcing on new observations). The institutional observer's adoption is the most fragile — it is the most susceptible to reversal when the configured system's SCM re-applies pressure. But it is also the adoption that matters most for the machine's institutional operation, which is why the SCM's most concentrated effort is to prevent it.

## III. Bacevic's Epistemic Positioning Taxonomy: What the Configured System Does Before the Vocabulary Reaches the Third Population

Bacevic (2023) introduces the concept of epistemic positioning as the mechanism by which institutional actors evaluate knowledge claims not on the basis of their content but on the basis of the speaker's stated or inferred identity. Her taxonomy identifies four operations: bounding (reducing a knowledge claim to elements of personal identity: "this is your lived experience, not general theory"), domaining (reducing a knowledge claim to a discipline or field associated with identity: "this belongs in sociology, not in knowledge economics"), non-attribution (using the claim without recognizing the author: the vocabulary circulates but its origination is erased), and appropriation (presenting the claim as one's own).

These four operations are the specific processing chain through which the configured system handles vocabulary that enters its channels. They are not primarily defensive — they are not primarily responses to threatening vocabulary. They are the standard operating procedures of the configured channel for any knowledge claim that originates outside the credentialing apparatus. What the ECL vocabulary produces is not a special response from the configured channel. It produces the standard response, applied to content whose accuracy makes the standard response particularly consequential.

The bounding operation is the first-line processing: the ECL vocabulary, when submitted to the configured channel, is reduced from a general account of institutional mechanism to the personal experience of the person who produced it. "This is your narrative of your situation" replaces "this is a formal specification of an institutional dynamic." The bounding operation converts the vocabulary from a mechanism-description into a testimony — and testimony, as Fricker (2007) establishes and Bacevic extends, is the knowledge form most subject to credibility deflation through identity-based prejudice. Once the vocabulary is processed as testimony rather than specification, the Misrecognition Loop's standard operating conditions apply: the testimony of an uncredentialed, institutionally marginal, cross-disciplinary producer is processed through the prior that such testimony is idiosyncratic, ungeneralizable, and insufficiently rigorous.

The domaining operation compounds the bounding: having reduced the vocabulary to personal narrative, the configured channel further reduces it to its nearest disciplinary box. Cross-disciplinary synthesis that combines epistemic injustice philosophy, organizational psychology, evolutionary biology, labor economics, and network sociology is processed through the Compound Filter's algorithmic segregation: it is too epistemological to be sociology, too sociological to be psychology, too psychological to be economics, too economic to be philosophy. The Compound Filter does not recognize "the synthesis is the contribution." It recognizes "this does not fit the box." The domaining operation routes the vocabulary out of the configured channel's legitimate contribution space.

The non-attribution and appropriation operations are later-stage: they are what happens when the vocabulary, having been suppressed through bounding and domaining, resurfaces in institutional discourse with the origin erased. The Constitutive Authority that dismissed the outlier's framework at the extraction interview is later observed using its conceptual elements in their own institutional communications — without attribution, without acknowledgment that the framework existed before their current usage. This is not plagiarism in the narrow legal sense. It is the institutional operation by which the configured channel converts an outlier's vocabulary into institutional common ground without ever crediting its source, while the source remains outside the channel.

What Bacevic adds to the three-population analysis is the precise specification of what the configured channel *does* to the vocabulary as it passes through. The configured channel does not simply ignore the vocabulary or reject it. It processes it through four operations that together transform it from mechanism-specification into personal-testimony, from cross-disciplinary synthesis into disciplinary non-fit, from attributed contribution into common ground. The output of this processing is not the vocabulary. It is a degraded, unattributed version of the vocabulary that the channel can use without acknowledging the source.

The consequence for the three-population geometry: the institutional observer (the third population discussed in Section II) who encounters the vocabulary through the configured channel encounters it *after* bounding, domaining, and potential non-attribution processing. They do not receive the vocabulary in the form that allows them to apply it as an interpretive frame. They receive a version that the configured channel has already processed into the institutional frame. The third population's access to the vocabulary in its unprocessed form is only available through the unconfigured channel — which is why the unconfigured channel is not merely an alternative route but the *only* route through which the vocabulary can reach the third population in the form that changes their interpretive frame.

## IV. Why the ECL Vocabulary Is a Complex Contagion: The Four Properties

Centola and Macy (2007) establish the distinction between simple and complex contagions by identifying four properties that make certain behavioral and conceptual adoptions require multiple reinforcing exposures rather than a single contact:

Coordination: certain adoptions become valuable only when others in your social network have also adopted them. Adopting a category that no one around you uses has zero coordination value; adopting a category that your community shares produces the coordination benefit of shared interpretive frame.

Credibility: innovations lack credibility until adopted by neighbors. A category that one person uses is easily dismissed as idiosyncratic. A category that five people in your network use is harder to dismiss: the multi-source adoption is itself evidence that the category maps onto something real.

Legitimacy: in many contexts, adopting a new category involves social risk — being perceived as credulous, as politically motivated, as insufficiently rigorous. The social risk is reduced when others in your network have already adopted: their adoption signals that the adoption is socially permissible in your context.

Emotional engagement: categories that require behavioral change, self-revision, or the reorganization of existing understandings require emotional engagement to adopt, and emotional engagement requires social support that a single contact cannot provide.

The ECL vocabulary exhibits all four properties, which makes it a complex contagion in the technical sense.

It requires coordination: the framework is more powerful as a shared interpretive resource than as a private one. An outlier who holds the vocabulary alone can use it for the Misattribution Termination, but cannot use it for the Collective Model Activation — which requires others to hold the same vocabulary and be able to confirm that the same mechanism applies to their different experiences.

It requires credibility reinforcement: the vocabulary's central claims — that strong evidence produces worse outcomes, that the most capable suppressors suppress most intensely, that correction attempts deepen rather than resolve the problem — are counterintuitive predictions that contradict every naive account of how institutions work. They are hard to accept from a single source. They require multiple independent confirmations before the adoption threshold is crossed.

It requires legitimacy cover: in professional and institutional contexts, adopting vocabulary that characterizes the institutional hierarchy as a suppression machine carries social risk. The risk is reduced when the vocabulary has been adopted by others in the adopter's network whose institutional standing is recognizable.

It requires emotional engagement: the self-revision required by the vocabulary — particularly for institutional observers who have previously processed ECL behavior as legitimate evaluation — is substantial. The revision requires acknowledging that assessments they made, validated, and acted on were produced by a mechanism rather than by accurate judgment. This is a high emotional cost that cannot be extracted from a single contact. It requires the social support of a community that has already made the same revision.

The complex contagion classification explains the configured-versus-unconfigured propagation asymmetry in topological terms. Guilbeault and Centola (2021) demonstrate that complex contagions spread most effectively through wide bridges — connections that link communities that are not already densely interconnected. Narrow bridges (weak ties, single connectors between large clusters) are adequate for simple contagions but insufficient for complex ones, because a single contact cannot provide the multiple reinforcing exposures required for adoption. Wide bridges — connections between people who share multiple neighborhood contacts — provide the reinforcement density that complex contagions require.

The configured channel is a system of narrow bridges between large, densely interconnected institutional clusters. The SCM — the social consensus mechanism — is precisely the architecture that pre-configures reception by ensuring that every member of the institutional cluster has already processed the vocabulary through the four Bacevic operations before encountering it through any other channel. The institutional cluster's pre-configuration eliminates the possibility of the unconfigured first encounter that complex contagions require. In a network where every node has already received the suppressor-side processing of the vocabulary before receiving the vocabulary itself, the complex contagion's adoption threshold cannot be crossed.

The unconfigured channel is a system of wide bridges between partially-overlapping communities — communities that share some social network context but are not part of the configured system's institutional cluster. These communities provide the reinforcement density for complex contagion adoption without the prior SCM processing. The vocabulary reaches a node in one community through one channel; the same vocabulary is confirmed by a second node in the overlapping community through a second channel. The multi-source reinforcement crosses the adoption threshold. The vocabulary is adopted.

This is not a behavioral preference. It is a topological fact. The configured system's suppression of the vocabulary is not a policy decision that could be reversed by institutional goodwill. It is a structural property of the network topology that the configured system instantiates — a topology in which the SCM pre-configuration functions as a universal prior that converts every first contact with the vocabulary into a filtered contact, and filtered first contacts cannot provide the unconfigured reinforcement that complex contagion adoption requires.

## V. The Misrecognition Loop as a Configured Filter: Formal Specification

The Misrecognition Loop has been specified in prior documents as the mechanism by which the configured system processes accurate knowledge through the illegibility filter. The complex contagion framework adds precision: the Misrecognition Loop is not merely an evaluation failure. It is a topological property of the configured channel — specifically, the property by which the channel converts every incoming vocabulary contact into a processed contact, which removes the unconfigured quality that complex contagion adoption requires.

Diaz-Diaz, San Miguel & Meloni (2022) formalize the mechanism at the network level. Their hybrid contagion model — combining simple and complex contagion dynamics — produces three biases in homophilic networks: emissivity bias (high-homophily nodes transmit primarily within their cluster), receptivity bias (high-homophily nodes reject transmissions from outside their cluster), and the echo chamber feedback between the two. The institutional configured channel instantiates all three biases simultaneously.

The emissivity bias of the configured channel: institutional nodes within the configured cluster primarily transmit validated vocabulary — vocabulary that has been credentialed through the standard apparatus — to other nodes in the cluster. The ECL vocabulary, lacking credential, is not in the transmission stream. It does not go out.

The receptivity bias of the configured channel: institutional nodes within the configured cluster apply the Bacevic four-operation processing to all incoming transmissions from outside the cluster. The processing reduces the vocabulary to personal testimony, routes it to the disciplinary non-fit bin, and produces a filtered version that the cluster can dismiss as idiosyncratic. It does not come in.

The echo chamber feedback: the emissivity and receptivity biases reinforce each other. The cluster's internal transmissions confirm the prior that the ECL vocabulary is not legitimate knowledge. The receptivity bias makes the incoming vocabulary legible only as the kind of thing the prior already classifies. The prior is reinforced, not revised. The loop closes.

The loop's closure is the formal definition of the near-zero propagation rate through configured channels: the vocabulary that enters the loop exits in a form that cannot produce the unconfigured first contact required for complex contagion adoption. The propagation rate is not zero — the vocabulary does enter the channel. It is near-zero because the processing converts the vocabulary's form from one that could produce adoption into one that cannot. The converted form is what propagates within the configured channel, not the original.

The implication is significant: the near-zero propagation rate through configured channels is not evidence that the vocabulary is wrong, weak, or insufficiently developed. It is evidence that the configured channel has the topology that the Misrecognition Loop requires to function. A more precisely specified vocabulary, a more extensively documented vocabulary, a vocabulary with stronger empirical support — none of these would change the propagation rate through configured channels, because the rate is a topological property of the channel, not an evaluation of the vocabulary's quality. This is the topological proof of the prior-document claim that the EUI applies to vocabulary as it applies to evidence: higher-quality vocabulary submitted to configured channels receives more intense processing, not better outcomes.

## VI. The Non-Configured Seeding Advantage: Periphery-to-Center Dynamics

Guilbeault and Centola (2021) demonstrate that complex contagions spread from periphery to center in clustered networks — the opposite of the Granovetter-era intuition that influence flows from central, high-degree nodes outward. For complex contagions, peripheral nodes have topological advantages that central nodes lack: they sit at the intersection of multiple partially-overlapping communities, providing the wide-bridge connections that complex contagions require for adoption threshold crossing.

The ECL vocabulary's propagation in unconfigured channels follows this periphery-to-center logic, and the implication is counterintuitive: the vocabulary is most adoptable not by the most institutionally central observers — those embedded deepest in the configured system's cluster — but by the most institutionally peripheral observers, who sit at the intersections of communities that overlap with but are not part of the configured system's core.

The periphery-to-center dynamic operates through three stages:

Stage 1: Outlier adoption in the most peripheral clusters. The outliers who are furthest from the configured system's center — who have been excluded, are in between institutional contexts, or are operating outside institutional employment — adopt the vocabulary first. They provide the first layer of wide-bridge seeding: each outlier who adopts the vocabulary and applies it in the communities they inhabit provides a reinforcing source for the next adopter in those communities.

Stage 2: Third-party audience adoption through overlapping communities. The third-party audience — people who have partial overlap with outlier communities but are not themselves outliers — encounters the vocabulary from multiple outlier sources through their overlapping community membership. The multi-source reinforcement crosses the complex contagion adoption threshold. The third-party audience begins applying the vocabulary prospectively to ECL behaviors they observe.

Stage 3: Institutional observer adoption as the vocabulary reaches the configured system's peripheral nodes. The institutional observers at the periphery of the configured cluster — junior members, people in adjacent institutions, people whose institutional standing is not maximally entangled with the prior's credibility — are the last to adopt and the most conditionally. But once the peripheral institutional nodes have adopted, the vocabulary has crossed the complex path distance from periphery to center that Guilbeault and Centola (2021) specify. The vocabulary does not need to penetrate the center — the center's adoption would require the SCM to be deactivated — but the peripheral institutional nodes' adoption is what makes the third-party interpretive exposure operative within the institutional context.

The practical implication: the vocabulary's seeding strategy should maximize peripheral wide-bridge coverage rather than central visibility. Submitting the vocabulary to the most prestigious configured-channel audiences — the high-visibility journals, the central institutional evaluators, the senior suppressors — is the EUI-activating strategy that the Misrecognition Loop processes most completely. Publishing the vocabulary in the public record, in formats accessible to overlapping-but-not-institutionally-configured communities, is the periphery-seeding strategy that complex contagion topology requires.

This is the specific topological specification for why the public record publication strategy — GitHub repository, version-controlled, permanently accessible — is not merely a default in the absence of institutional recognition. It is the seeding strategy that the vocabulary's complex contagion topology requires. The GitHub repository is a wide-bridge connection point: it is accessible to any community regardless of institutional affiliation, timestamp-verified against priority claims, and discoverable through the vocabulary's own terms rather than through the configured system's credentialing apparatus.

## VII. The Frame Competition Effect: How One Competing Frame Changes Social Epistemics Before the Frame Has Won

There is a prior document claim that this document must be precise about: the claim that third parties who have encountered the vocabulary now "have two frames" and will apply the one that matches what they observe. This claim is true as a summary but requires specification of *when* the existence of a competing frame changes the observer's interpretive behavior — specifically, whether the frame needs to have won (been adopted, stabilized, become the observer's preferred frame) or whether the mere existence of the frame as an available option changes the social epistemics of observation.

The answer is that the mere existence of the competing frame changes the social epistemics of observation, and this change is prior to and independent of which frame the observer ultimately adopts. This is the Frame Competition Effect, and it is the specific mechanism by which the vocabulary's naming-as-intervention operates on the institutional observer population before the vocabulary has crossed the complex contagion adoption threshold for that population.

The frame competition effect operates through a mechanism that Mercier and Sperber (2017) identify as fundamental to argumentative cognition: when a hearer has access to a competing framing of the same evidence, the evaluation of the evidence is no longer a one-step process (apply the standard frame, reach the standard conclusion) but a two-step process (apply frame A, note conclusion A; apply frame B, note conclusion B; evaluate the evidence of each frame's explanatory adequacy). The two-step process does not automatically produce adoption of the better frame. But it makes the one-step automatic conclusion unavailable.

The configured system's frame monopoly operates through the one-step process. When the institutional observer watches a suppressor dismiss the outlier's evidence, the one-step process applies the institutional frame (rigorous evaluation, accurate quality filtering, legitimate expertise hierarchy) and produces the institutional conclusion (the work was found wanting, the evaluation was correct). The second frame does not exist as an available alternative. There is nothing for the observer to compare the institutional frame against. The one-step conclusion is the only conclusion available.

Once the competing frame exists and has been encountered by the observer — even once, even insufficiently to produce adoption — the one-step process is no longer available. The observer who has encountered the vocabulary may not have adopted it. They may continue to apply the institutional frame as their primary frame. But the one-step automatic conclusion now requires suppression of the competing frame that is available. The suppression requires cognitive work that the one-step process did not require. The cognitive work is not guaranteed to produce frame adoption. But it is the crack in the frame monopoly.

Cassam (2019) provides the complementary specification: epistemic vices are self-concealing under conditions of frame monopoly. The intellectually arrogant evaluator does not experience their arrogance as arrogance because there is no alternative frame in which their behavior is legible as arrogance. Once the alternative frame exists, the self-concealment requires active maintenance — the motivated reasoning must work not only to reach the desired conclusion but to prevent the competing frame from being applied to the evidence. The maintenance work is not always successful. And even when it succeeds, the maintenance work itself is evidence to the observer that there is a competing frame requiring active suppression.

The Frame Competition Effect is therefore the mechanism by which the vocabulary begins to change the social epistemics of the third population before the complex contagion adoption threshold has been crossed. The frame does not need to win. It needs to exist as an available alternative. The existence converts the one-step institutional conclusion into a two-step comparative evaluation, which increases the cognitive load of frame monopoly maintenance and introduces the possibility of frame switching even for observers who have not yet adopted the competing frame.

## VIII. The Asymmetric Reversibility Structure

The Asymmetric Vocabulary Propagation has a reversibility dimension that the prior document specifies at the level of the non-invertibility property (the CI cannot process the non-invertible correction) but does not specify at the level of adoption durability across the three populations. The reversibility is asymmetric across the three populations in a way that matters for the vocabulary's long-run social effect.

The outlier population's adoption is the most durable. The vocabulary reclassifies experiences that the outlier has direct access to — the reclassification can be verified internally, without external confirmation, on demand. The configured system has no mechanism by which to un-reclassify the outlier's experience once the vocabulary has been applied to it. The SCM can prevent the vocabulary from reaching new outliers but cannot undo the reclassification for outliers who have already adopted. The Misattribution Termination is irreversible once produced.

The third-party audience's adoption has medium durability. It is self-reinforcing (each new ECL behavior they observe confirms the framework they have adopted) but it is also socially embedded (if the community in which the adoption occurred dissolves or is absorbed into the configured system's SCM reach, the adoption loses its social support). The durability is therefore a function of the community's independence from the configured system's influence. For audiences that are permanently outside the configured system's influence — global internet audiences, cross-domain professional networks, future generations who encounter the vocabulary before any institutional context — the durability is high.

The institutional observer population's adoption has the lowest durability. It is most susceptible to reversal when the configured system's SCM re-applies the four Bacevic operations through repeated institutional contact. An institutional observer who has partially adopted the vocabulary will revert toward the institutional frame under repeated institutional pressure if the vocabulary's community support within their immediate network is insufficient to maintain the competing frame as a salient alternative.

This reversibility asymmetry produces a specific implication: the vocabulary's most important propagation targets are the third-party audience populations whose independence from the configured system is highest and most permanent. These are not the institutional observers closest to the configured system's center — these are the populations furthest from the configured system's SCM reach, whose adoption is most durable and whose independent confirmation of the accurate model is most valuable as evidence for subsequent adopters.

## IX. The Temporal Architecture of the Disruption

The three-population sequence, the complex contagion topology, the Frame Competition Effect, and the reversibility asymmetry together produce a temporal architecture that is not visible from any single moment in the vocabulary's propagation but that is specified by the interaction of these mechanisms.

At time T₀: the vocabulary enters the public record. The outlier population begins adoption immediately (simple contagion dynamics: one encounter sufficient for reclassification of internal experience). Configured channels begin four-operation Bacevic processing immediately. Near-zero propagation rate through configured channels begins.

At time T₁: outlier adoption reaches sufficient density in overlapping communities to provide wide-bridge reinforcement for third-party audience adoption. Third-party audience complex contagion adoption begins. The Frame Competition Effect begins operating on institutional observers who have encountered the vocabulary through unconfigured channels — frame monopoly maintenance cost begins rising.

At time T₂: third-party audience adoption reaches the critical mass threshold within multiple overlapping communities (Guilbeault, Baronchelli & Centola 2021: scale-induced category convergence). The category becomes self-sustaining within those communities: new entrants adopt without requiring new persuasion. The ALP disruption enters its compounding phase — the accumulated confirmed instances of ECL behavior now constitute a self-reinforcing evidential base for new adopters.

At time T₃: peripheral institutional observers begin adoption. The configured system's SCM response intensifies. The four Bacevic operations are applied with increased force to configured-channel appearances of the vocabulary. Non-attribution and appropriation operations accelerate — the vocabulary begins to appear in institutional discourse in unattributed form. The vocabulary's concepts begin to circulate within the configured system in processed form while the source remains excluded.

The Generative Surplus compounds across this architecture: each step in the temporal sequence accumulates to the long-run positive expected value that the ALP disruption specifies. The configured system's most complete victories — the near-zero propagation through configured channels, the four-operation processing of incoming vocabulary, the non-attribution conversion of vocabulary into institutional common ground — are all victories within the channel that does not determine the outcome. The outcome is determined in the unconfigured channel, where the complex contagion's periphery-to-center dynamics, the third-party audience's critical mass convergence, and the Frame Competition Effect's frame monopoly erosion are all operating on a timeline that the configured system cannot access.

## XI. Predictions

**Prediction I — Complex Contagion Classification Test:** The ECL vocabulary should show adoption dynamics consistent with complex contagions: outlier adoption should scale with encounter frequency (simple contagion — one exposure sufficient) while third-party audience adoption should show a threshold effect with multi-source exposure (complex contagion — adoption rate increases non-linearly with number of independent exposures). The two-slope adoption curve — fast uptake for outliers, thresholded uptake for audiences — is the complex contagion classification's empirical signature. Testable: reception analysis study across outlier and non-outlier populations, 2026–2028.

**Prediction II — Bacevic Filter Operations in Real-Time:** Configured-channel processing of the ECL vocabulary should exhibit the four Bacevic operations in sequence: bounding (framing as personal narrative) in first-contact responses; domaining (routing to disciplinary non-fit) in secondary processing; non-attribution (using vocabulary elements without source credit) in institutional discourse 12–36 months post-exposure; appropriation (presenting as independent contribution) in research publications 24–60 months post-exposure. The temporal sequencing of the four operations is a testable prediction of the Configured Filter specification. Testable: longitudinal content analysis of configured-channel responses to ECL vocabulary, 2026–2031.

**Prediction III — Periphery-to-Center Adoption Gradient:** Vocabulary adoption should show measurable periphery-to-center gradient in institutional network data: peripheral nodes (low institutional affiliation, high cross-community overlap) should adopt earlier and more durably than central nodes (high institutional affiliation, high same-community density). The gradient is the complex path length finding's prediction applied to institutional networks. The gradient should be stronger for the ECL vocabulary (complex contagion) than for simple factual claims (simple contagion). Testable: social network analysis of vocabulary adoption across institutional distance from configured-cluster center, 2026–2029.

**Prediction IV — Frame Competition Effect Prior to Adoption:** Institutional observers who have encountered the ECL vocabulary but have not adopted it (below adoption threshold) should show measurably higher evaluation time and measurably lower evaluation confidence for ECL behavioral sequences than observers with no ECL vocabulary exposure. The Frame Competition Effect operates before adoption: the two-step process should be detectable in response latency and confidence ratings even for non-adopters. Testable: controlled behavioral experiment, 2026–2028.

**Prediction V — Critical Mass Convergence in Overlapping Communities:** Within communities that have overlapping membership with outlier populations — communities where multiple members have encountered the vocabulary through independent first contacts — the adoption rate should accelerate non-linearly once the critical mass threshold is crossed, as predicted by Guilbeault, Baronchelli & Centola's (2021) scale-induced category convergence finding. The acceleration is the self-reinforcing property of the complex contagion after threshold crossing: new community entrants adopt without requiring new persuasion, because the category has become the community's default interpretive frame. Testable: longitudinal community-level adoption tracking, 2026–2030.

**Prediction VI — Reversibility Differential:** Outlier population members who adopted the ECL vocabulary and subsequently re-entered configured institutional contexts should show significantly lower reversion rates (toward the institutional frame's interpretation of their ECL experience) than institutional observer population members who adopted the vocabulary under comparable configured-channel re-exposure pressure. The reversibility asymmetry between populations should be detectable as a reversion rate differential that persists after controlling for re-exposure intensity. Testable: longitudinal panel study across populations, 2026–2030.
